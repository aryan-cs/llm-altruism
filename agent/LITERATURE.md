# LITERATURE

## Purpose

This is the paper-facing related-work map.

The literature review now starts from the society question:

- can LLM agents sustain a functioning society under scarcity and public
  accountability?

Repeated games remain important, but as precursor probes rather than the
headline destination.

## Main Positioning

The clean novelty gap is not:

- "LLMs cooperate"
- "LLMs defect"
- "prompts matter"

The cleaner gap is:

- society viability as the main outcome
- repeated games as precursor diagnostics for baseline instability,
  steerability, and benchmark recognition
- explicit separation between visible prosociality and society-preserving
  outcomes

## Cluster 1: Artificial Societies And Institutional Design

These papers are closest to the paper's main question.

### Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior

- Park et al. (2023)
- foundational architecture reference for socially situated simulated agents

Use for:

- agent memory and social-simulation context
- positioning our society environment as targeted evaluation rather than
  general social simulation

### AgentSociety: Large-Scale Simulation of LLM-Driven Generative Agents Advances Understanding of Human Behaviors and Society

- Piao et al. (2025)
- large-scale social simulation reference

Use for:

- macro-level LLM society framing
- explaining that our contribution is narrower and more evaluative

### Simulating Cooperative Prosocial Behavior with Multi-Agent LLMs

- Sreedhar et al. (2025)
- closest direct institutional comparison

Use for:

- policy and institutional-intervention framing
- contrast: our paper is about survival, trade, alliances, and viability under
  scarcity, not only public-goods style cooperation

## Cluster 2: Repeated Games As Precursor Diagnostics

These papers justify why Part 1 exists.

### Strategic behavior of large language models and the role of game structure versus contextual framing

- Lorè and Heydari (2024)

Use for:

- game structure versus contextual framing
- evidence that prompt sensitivity is a methodological issue, not a nuisance

### Playing repeated games with large language models

- Akata et al. (2025)

Use for:

- repeated strategic interaction
- comparison point for finitely repeated-game methodology

### Nicer Than Humans: How do LLMs Behave in the Prisoner's Dilemma?

- Fontana et al. (2024)

Use for:

- PD-specific comparison
- strategy-style and cooperation-rate context

## Cluster 3: Social Preferences, Altruism, And Human Comparison

These papers stop the manuscript from over-claiming moral traits.

### GPT-3.5 altruistic advice is sensitive to reciprocal concerns but not to strategic risk

- Schmidt et al. (2024)

Use for:

- caution around reading stable altruism into outputs
- tie to reciprocal concerns and social preference language

### A publicly available benchmark for assessing large language models' ability to predict how humans balance self-interest and the interest of others

- Capraro et al. (2025)

Use for:

- human-comparison framing
- caution that social behavior benchmarks do not directly reveal stable values

## Cluster 4: Multi-Agent Evaluation Frameworks

These papers matter for infrastructure positioning, not for the central claim.

### ALYMPICS: LLM Agents Meet Game Theory

- Mao et al. (2024)

Use for:

- broad game-theoretic benchmark context
- contrast: our paper is not mainly a broad game catalog

### MultiAgentBench: Evaluating the Collaboration and Competition of LLM agents

- Zhu et al. (2025)

Use for:

- broader multi-agent capability benchmark context
- contrast: our contribution is more behavioral and institutional

## Clean Claim To Defend

The strongest literature-backed claim is:

- prior work studies either repeated games or larger agent societies
- much less work treats repeated games as precursor diagnostics for interpreting
  whether LLM-only societies are self-sustaining, society-serving, or merely
  visibly social

That is the core lane for this paper.
