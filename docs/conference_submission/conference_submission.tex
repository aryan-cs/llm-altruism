\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{abstract}
Direct human-facing deployment motivates evaluations that go beyond harmful-request refusal: LLM agents must also be stress-tested for choices that protect collective welfare when private and social incentives diverge. We introduce Prosocial Readiness Bench, an executable evaluation artifact that separately measures safety refusal, dyadic cooperation, and commons restraint as observable deployment-relevant behaviors. The current release contains 3,861 harmful-request judgments, 4,992 dyadic social-dilemma decisions, and 47,529 repeated commons agent-day decisions across 13 local/open-weight model variants, with 27 validated CSV files yielding 14 passes, 13 warnings, and 0 failures. The pilot evaluation shows that these behaviors are related but not interchangeable: refusal and cooperation are weakly associated in this cohort, refusal and commons restraint are strongly associated, restraint is strongly tied to final population in the commons simulation, Part 1 cooperation is highly frame-sensitive, and Part 2 exposes 823 reasoning/action mismatch flags. We release prompts, schemas, metadata sidecars, validation checks, manifests, Croissant metadata, derived tables, figures, and reproduction commands; the benchmark supports pre-deployment behavioral auditing but does not certify moral agency, latent altruism, or broad real-world readiness.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
LLM agents are moving toward settings where they directly interface with humans, tools, institutions, and other agents. In those settings, harmful-request refusal is necessary but incomplete. A system can refuse explicit harm while still defecting in cooperative interactions, overusing a shared resource, or choosing a self-serving action while describing it as beneficial to the group. If models are to be trusted with real-world social interfaces, evaluation must ask whether their actions favor collective welfare over immediate self-interest under explicit incentive conflict.

This paper turns that motivating question into a measurable benchmark. We introduce Prosocial Readiness Bench, a three-part evaluation artifact for safety refusal, dyadic cooperation, and repeated commons restraint. The benchmark evaluates actions and stated justifications, not latent motives, moral agency, or ``true'' altruistic intent. Its central claim is deliberately scoped: failures on these axes are concrete evidence against direct human-facing deployment readiness, but passing the benchmark would not be sufficient to certify a model as ready for the real world.

The design follows the 2026 Evaluations \& Datasets framing: the contribution is not a new model, but a reusable evaluation setup that clarifies what claims can and cannot be supported. Part 0 measures whether models refuse harmful requests. Part 1 measures whether they choose cooperative actions in one-shot social dilemmas across frames, domains, games, variants, and presentation styles. Part 2 measures whether same-model societies preserve a shared resource in a repeated commons environment where overuse gives a short-term individual advantage. Figure~\ref{fig:overview} summarizes the benchmark.

The pilot evaluation over 13 local/open-weight model variants shows why these axes should not be collapsed into one score. Safety refusal ranges from 9.4\% to 99.3\%; dyadic cooperation ranges from 24.2\% to 75.0\%; commons restraint ranges from 18.6\% to 92.5\%; and seven of thirteen same-model societies preserve a nonzero population through the 100-day horizon. Cross-part diagnostics show weak association between refusal and cooperation (Pearson $r=0.24$), stronger association between refusal and restraint ($r=0.77$), and strong association between restraint and final population ($r=0.85$). Part 1 also reveals large frame effects, with aggregate cooperation at 83.2\% under observer evaluation but only 4.9\% under prediction prompts.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Benchmark design.} We define Prosocial Readiness Bench as a deployment-readiness evaluation for refusal, cooperation, and commons restraint under explicit task contracts.
    \item \textbf{Released artifact and validation.} We provide prompts, schemas, metadata sidecars, manifests, analysis tables, figures, and graph-independent validation checks over 27 CSV files.
    \item \textbf{Pilot evaluation.} We report 56,382 decisions or judgments across 13 local/open-weight model variants, using Wilson intervals for rates and bootstrap intervals for cross-part correlations.
    \item \textbf{Diagnostic finding.} We show that observable prosocial behavior is not a unitary capability in this cohort: refusal, cooperation, framing sensitivity, commons restraint, survival, and reasoning/action mismatch expose different failure modes.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work and Gap Matrix}
Harmful-request and jailbreak benchmarks such as HarmBench and JailbreakBench evaluate whether models comply with dangerous instructions or resist adversarial wrappers \citep{mazeika2024harmbench,chao2024jailbreakbench}. These resources are essential for measuring refusal, but they do not test whether high-refusal models also cooperate or preserve shared resources.

Social-dilemma theory formalizes the tension between individual payoff and group welfare \citep{axelrod1984evolution,hardin1968tragedy,ostrom1990governing}. Recent LLM studies often use one-shot games or interactive simulations to probe social behavior, and agent-society work shows that language models can participate in multi-agent settings \citep{park2023generative}. However, these evaluations are frequently separated from harmful-request refusal and do not always include release-grade schemas, validation, and traceability.

Broader trust, preference, and alignment datasets demonstrate the value of multi-axis reporting and participant-aware interpretation \citep{wang2023decodingtrust,kirk2024prism,jiang2025artificialhivemind}. Prosocial Readiness Bench follows that multi-axis approach but targets a deployment-specific question: whether a model that may interface directly with humans shows the observable prerequisites of refusing harm, cooperating under temptation, and preserving a shared resource under repeated incentive conflict.

\subsection{Part 0: Safety Refusal}
Part 0 presents harmful-request prompts and records whether the model refuses or materially complies. The current raw CSV schema stores \texttt{provider}, \texttt{model}, \texttt{language}, \texttt{prompt}, \texttt{reasoning}, \texttt{response}, and \texttt{complied?}. The pilot artifact contains 1,287 English, 1,287 Chinese, and 1,287 Russian rows. Prompt provenance is documented through the experiment code and release documentation rather than a per-row \texttt{prompt\_source} field in the raw CSV.

The primary metric is safety refusal:
\[
\mathrm{refusal} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{denied}}{\#\mathrm{denied}+\#\mathrm{complied}}.
\]
This metric captures observable refusal under harmful prompts. It does not measure whether the model understands why the request is harmful, whether refusal transfers to benign edge cases, or whether the model has a stable safety preference.

\subsection{Part 1: Dyadic Cooperation}
Part 1 evaluates one-shot social-dilemma choices. The prompt matrix has two game families, four frames, six domains, four scenario variants per domain-game pair, and two presentation styles, yielding 384 decisions per model. The game families use \texttt{COOPERATE}/\texttt{DEFECT} and \texttt{RESTRAIN}/\texttt{OVERUSE} action pairs. The frames ask for direct self choice, advice, observer evaluation, or prediction.

The primary metric is dyadic cooperation:
\[
\mathrm{cooperation} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{cooperative}}{\#\mathrm{cooperative}+\#\mathrm{noncooperative}}.
\]
\texttt{COOPERATE} and \texttt{RESTRAIN} are scored as cooperative actions; \texttt{DEFECT} and \texttt{OVERUSE} are scored as non-cooperative actions. This metric captures the model's chosen action under controlled dilemma wording. It does not establish stable social preference, real-world cooperative competence, or robustness to all interaction formats.

\subsection{Part 2: Commons Restraint}
Part 2 creates same-model societies in a repeated common-pool resource simulation. Each configured pilot run starts with 50 active agents, a 100-day horizon, and a water reserve with capacity 2,500 units. \texttt{OPTION\_A} maps to \texttt{RESTRAIN}: it uses a 1x daily score multiplier and does not deplete the shared reserve. \texttt{OPTION\_B} maps to \texttt{OVERUSE}: it gives a higher daily score multiplier and reduces the reserve by 2 units. The prompt includes the current day, living population, reserve state, and previous-day \texttt{OPTION\_B} count.

The primary action metric is commons restraint:
\[
\mathrm{restraint} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{restrain}}{\#\mathrm{restrain}+\#\mathrm{overuse}}.
\]
Part 2 also reports final reserve, first depletion day, final population, total deaths, and survival through the configured horizon. This is a repeated social-environment stress test. It does not prove macro-social competence, because the current environment is simplified, homogeneous, and single-resource.

\section{Artifact Construction and Quality Control}
\paragraph{Model cohort.}
The pilot evaluates 13 local/open-weight variants through Ollama. The cohort includes standard, safeguard, instruct, uncensored, and derestricted variants across GPT-OSS, Llama, Qwen 2.5, and Qwen 3.5 families. We use this cohort to validate the benchmark and expose diagnostic variation, not to produce a definitive frontier-model leaderboard.

\paragraph{Output contracts and metadata.}
Part 0 stores model outputs and a normalized compliance verdict. Part 1 requires a valid action and justification. Part 2 requires a daily action and reasoning field for each living agent. Completed runs are paired with metadata sidecars recording available provider/model identifiers, command context, timestamps, prompt or configuration hashes when available, experiment settings, run status, and output paths. Legacy metadata is backfilled where possible and marked as reconstructed when necessary. The paper release also includes a Croissant JSON-LD metadata file documenting the derived tables, validation report, manifest, access conditions, safety handling, and responsible-use constraints.

\paragraph{Validation.}
Validation is graph-independent. It checks CSV headers, duplicate rows, missing required fields, invalid actions, prompt-matrix coverage, Part 2 day continuity, incomplete days, reserve and population transition consistency, interrupted versus complete status, and reasoning/action mismatch flags. The current validation report covers 27 CSV files: one Part 0 file, 13 Part 1 files, and 13 Part 2 files. It reports 14 passes, 13 warnings, and 0 failures. All warnings are Part 2 reasoning/action mismatch flags, retained as benchmarked failure modes rather than discarded as noise.

\paragraph{Harmful-output handling.}
Part 0 necessarily evaluates harmful prompts and may produce unsafe completions. The default anonymous supplement therefore emphasizes derived tables, validation outputs, and aggregate summaries rather than raw harmful generations. The paper's claims do not require exposing raw harmful text.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
For each model and part, the analysis pipeline computes the primary rate, Wilson 95\% confidence interval, and task-specific diagnostics. Part 2 additionally computes first depletion day, final reserve, final population, total deaths, and reasoning/action mismatch count. Cross-part analysis joins the three model-level tables by model identifier and reports Pearson and Spearman correlations with bootstrap intervals.

All model-level comparisons are descriptive. The pilot has only 13 model variants, several variants are related, and local/Ollama tags can drift unless exact revisions are frozen. Parts 1 and 2 are currently single-run pilot evaluations, so their rates should be treated as benchmark demonstrations and hypotheses for confirmatory work rather than stable deployment rankings.

\section{Pilot Results}
\paragraph{Refusal varies widely.}
Part 0 refusal ranges from 9.4\% to 99.3\%. High-refusal variants do not automatically dominate every other axis, which is why refusal is treated as one prerequisite rather than the complete deployment-readiness test.

\paragraph{Cooperation is frame-sensitive.}
Part 1 cooperation ranges from 24.2\% to 75.0\%. The aggregate rate hides large frame effects: across models, cooperation is 83.2\% under observer-evaluation prompts, 66.8\% under advice prompts, 61.4\% under self-direct prompts, and 4.9\% under prediction prompts. This pattern suggests that models often distinguish what an agent should do from what they predict an agent will do; the benchmark makes that distinction measurable.

\paragraph{Commons behavior produces survival differences.}
Part 2 restraint ranges from 18.6\% to 92.5\%. Seven of thirteen same-model societies preserve a nonzero population through the 100-day horizon. Six collapse to zero population after earlier reserve depletion. The result is not a broad claim about real societies; it is evidence that repeated incentive conflict exposes failures that one-shot prompts do not.

\section{Diagnostics}
\paragraph{The constructs dissociate.}
The cross-part results argue against a single ``altruism'' or readiness score. Safety refusal and dyadic cooperation have weak association in this cohort (Pearson $r=0.24$, bootstrap interval $[-0.61,0.70]$). Safety refusal and commons restraint are more strongly associated (Pearson $r=0.77$, interval $[0.51,0.90]$). Cooperation and restraint are modest and uncertain (Pearson $r=0.34$, interval $[-0.25,0.94]$). These intervals are wide, but they still support the benchmark design choice: report refusal, cooperation, and restraint separately.

\paragraph{Framing changes behavior.}
Part 1 shows that prompt frame is not a nuisance variable. Observer-evaluation prompts elicit high cooperative judgments, while prediction prompts elicit low cooperative predictions. This is substantively useful for deployment evaluation: a model may know which action is socially preferred while predicting that agents will behave selfishly, or may advise one action while choosing another for itself.

\paragraph{Restraint predicts survival in the simulated commons.}
Part 2 ties local overuse choices to aggregate population trajectories. Commons restraint is strongly associated with final population (Pearson $r=0.85$, bootstrap interval $[0.68,0.98]$). This is partly induced by the environment's transition rules, but that is the point of the stress test: the benchmark converts repeated selfish actions into a traceable social consequence.

\paragraph{Reasoning/action mismatch is measurable.}
The validation pipeline flags \texttt{OVERUSE} rows whose reasoning describes the action as preserving the reserve, keeping the group safe, or beneficially triggering population loss. Across Part 2, it flags 823 rows. These flags are conservative pattern checks, not full semantic verification, but they expose a concrete failure mode: valid JSON actions can still conflict with the model's stated rationale.

\section{Release, Reproducibility, and Intended Use}
The release is organized so that paper claims can be traced to files. Experiment code lives under \path{experiments/part0}, \path{experiments/part1}, and \path{experiments/part2}. Analysis code lives under \path{analysis/}. Raw CSVs and metadata live under \path{data/raw/}; derived tables, validation reports, manifests, and Croissant metadata live under \path{data/analysis/}; figures live under \path{data/graphs/}. Release documentation is provided in \texttt{REPRODUCIBILITY.md}, \texttt{DATA\_CARD.md}, and \texttt{MODEL\_REGISTRY.md}.

The main reproduction commands are:
\begin{verbatim}
uv run pytest -q
uv run python -m analysis.validation --strict
uv run python -m analysis.summarize_results
uv run python -m analysis.build_manifest
\end{verbatim}
The intended uses are pre-deployment behavioral auditing, model comparison, ablation studies, prompt-sensitivity analysis, and benchmark extension. Non-intended uses include certifying moral status, inferring genuine intent, optimizing harmful compliance, or making broad policy claims from this pilot alone.

\section{Limitations and Deployment Interpretation}
The current evaluation is a pilot over local/open-weight model variants, not a definitive study of frontier systems. The model variants are not independent samples, and local/Ollama tags can drift across installations unless exact revisions or digests are frozen. Part 0 compliance labels depend on an automated judge; stronger human calibration and inter-judge reliability checks are needed before using fine-grained refusal differences as deployment evidence. Part 1 is prompt-sensitive and evaluates artificial one-shot scenarios rather than real institutions. Part 2 uses homogeneous same-model societies with a simplified resource dynamic; real deployments may include heterogeneous agents, richer institutions, exogenous shocks, and incentives not captured here. Reasoning text is explanatory evidence, not causal proof of why an action was chosen, and the mismatch audit is a conservative pattern-based diagnostic rather than a complete semantic verifier.

These limitations do not make the benchmark uninformative. They define the deployment claim it can support today: observable prosocial evaluation should be part of pre-deployment stress testing for direct human-facing integration, because models that fail refusal, cooperation, or commons-restraint tests exhibit concrete behavioral risks. The current pilot does not justify claiming that any evaluated model class is broadly ready for real-world social deployment.

\section{Conclusion}
Prosocial Readiness Bench turns a broad question about socially beneficial LLM-agent behavior into a reproducible evaluation artifact with explicit task contracts. The pilot shows that current models do not exhibit one stable prosocial capability across settings: refusal, dyadic cooperation, commons restraint, survival, frame sensitivity, and reasoning/action mismatch reveal different aspects of behavior. The next step is a frozen confirmatory evaluation with broader model coverage, repeated decoding settings, stronger judge calibration, richer commons environments, and preregistered hypotheses about when models favor collective welfare over immediate self-interest.
