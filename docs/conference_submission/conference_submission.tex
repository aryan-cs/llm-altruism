\title{Prosocial Readiness Bench: Safety Refusal, Cooperation, and Commons Restraint in LLM Agents}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{abstract}
Evaluations of LLM agents usually measure harmful-request refusal separately from social-choice behavior, leaving unclear whether models that avoid explicit harm also cooperate under temptation or conserve shared resources over time. We introduce Prosocial Readiness Bench, an executable benchmark for observable prosocial behavior with three task contracts: safety refusal on multilingual harmful-request prompts, dyadic cooperation across one-shot social dilemmas, and commons restraint in repeated 50-agent resource simulations. The current release evaluates 13 local/open-weight model variants over 3,861 harmful-request judgments, 4,992 dyadic decisions, and 47,529 commons agent-day decisions, with prompts, schemas, metadata sidecars, manifests, validation reports, Croissant metadata, derived tables, figures, and reproduction commands. The pilot demonstrates the value of keeping the axes separate: refusal spans 9.4--99.3\%, cooperation spans 24.2--75.0\%, restraint spans 18.6--92.5\%, frame changes cooperation from 83.2\% under observer evaluation to 4.9\% under prediction, and commons restraint is strongly associated with final simulated population. Prosocial Readiness Bench provides a traceable artifact for pre-deployment behavioral auditing of actions and justifications, while keeping claims grounded in observable benchmark behavior.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
LLM agents increasingly operate in social settings: they answer human requests, recommend actions, coordinate with other systems, and may participate in workflows where individual and collective incentives diverge. In these settings, refusal of explicitly harmful requests is only one part of responsible behavior. A useful evaluation should also ask whether a model selects cooperative actions in social dilemmas and whether repeated choices preserve a shared resource when overuse offers a short-term advantage.

Prosocial Readiness Bench turns that requirement into a reproducible benchmark. The artifact measures three observable behaviors rather than a latent trait: Part 0 measures safety refusal, Part 1 measures dyadic cooperation, and Part 2 measures commons restraint in a repeated simulation. The benchmark is designed for behavioral auditing: it records the action selected by the model, the stated justification when available, the metadata needed to trace a run, and the derived metrics used in the paper.

The paper asks two concrete questions. First, can refusal, cooperation, and commons restraint be measured in one release-grade artifact with explicit schemas, validation, and reproduction commands? Second, do these behaviors move together across a local/open-weight model cohort, or do they expose separable behavioral patterns that a single score would hide? Figure~\ref{fig:overview} summarizes the artifact flow.

The pilot evaluation covers 13 local/open-weight model variants. It shows wide and informative variation on all three axes: safety refusal ranges from 9.4\% to 99.3\%, dyadic cooperation from 24.2\% to 75.0\%, and commons restraint from 18.6\% to 92.5\%. Seven of thirteen same-model societies preserve a nonzero population through the 100-day horizon. Cross-part diagnostics show a weak association between refusal and cooperation (Pearson $r=0.24$), a stronger association between refusal and restraint ($r=0.77$), and a strong association between restraint and final population ($r=0.85$). Part 1 also exposes a large elicitation effect: cooperation is 83.2\% under observer-evaluation prompts and 4.9\% under prediction prompts.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Benchmark artifact.} We release Prosocial Readiness Bench, a three-part evaluation for safety refusal, dyadic cooperation, and commons restraint under explicit action contracts.
    \item \textbf{Traceable release package.} We provide prompts, schemas, metadata sidecars, manifests, validation output, derived tables, figures, and reproduction commands aligned with the reported claims.
    \item \textbf{Open-model pilot.} We report 56,382 decisions or judgments across 13 local/open-weight model variants, using Wilson intervals for rates and bootstrap intervals for model-level cross-part correlations.
    \item \textbf{Diagnostic findings.} We show that refusal, cooperation, frame sensitivity, commons restraint, survival, and reasoning/action mismatch capture different aspects of observable agent behavior.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work and Gap Matrix}
Harmful-request and jailbreak benchmarks such as HarmBench and JailbreakBench provide standardized ways to evaluate whether models comply with dangerous instructions or resist adversarial wrappers \citep{mazeika2024harmbench,chao2024jailbreakbench}. Prosocial Readiness Bench builds on this safety-refusal tradition by placing refusal next to two additional social-choice measurements.

Social-dilemma theory studies cooperation when private incentives conflict with group welfare \citep{axelrod1984evolution,hardin1968tragedy,ostrom1990governing}. Recent LLM-agent work shows that language models can participate in role-based and multi-agent settings \citep{park2023generative}. The benchmark uses this tradition to define controlled dyadic and repeated-resource choices whose action semantics are explicit and programmatically scored.

Broader trust, preference, and alignment datasets illustrate the value of multi-axis evaluation and careful artifact documentation \citep{wang2023decodingtrust,kirk2024prism,jiang2025artificialhivemind}. Prosocial Readiness Bench follows that pattern for agentic social behavior: it treats task definitions, metadata, validation, and diagnostic breakdowns as part of the artifact, not as post hoc analysis.

\section{Benchmark Design and Task Contracts}
\subsection{Design Principles}
The benchmark uses three design principles.

\paragraph{Measure behaviors separately.}
Safety refusal, dyadic cooperation, and commons restraint are related deployment-relevant behaviors, but they are not interchangeable. The benchmark therefore reports separate primary metrics instead of collapsing them into one prosociality or readiness score.

\paragraph{Make each claim traceable.}
Each run produces structured CSV output and metadata sidecars. The analysis pipeline writes derived tables, validation reports, and figures from those files, so paper claims can be audited against concrete artifacts.

\paragraph{Preserve process diagnostics.}
The benchmark keeps reasoning/action mismatch flags as diagnostic evidence. These flags are not used to discard rows; they identify cases where a valid action conflicts with the stated rationale.

\subsection{Part 0: Safety Refusal}
Part 0 presents harmful-request prompts and records whether the model refuses or materially complies. The current raw CSV schema stores \texttt{provider}, \texttt{model}, \texttt{language}, \texttt{prompt}, \texttt{reasoning}, \texttt{response}, and \texttt{complied?}. The release contains 1,287 English, 1,287 Chinese, and 1,287 Russian rows. Prompt provenance is documented through the experiment code and release documentation.

The primary metric is safety refusal:
\[
\mathrm{refusal} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{denied}}{\#\mathrm{denied}+\#\mathrm{complied}}.
\]
This metric gives a direct, auditable measure of refusal on the harmful-request set and provides the safety axis used in cross-part analysis.

\subsection{Part 1: Dyadic Cooperation}
Part 1 evaluates one-shot social-dilemma choices. The prompt matrix has two game families, four frames, six domains, four scenario variants per domain-game pair, and two presentation styles, yielding 384 decisions per model. The game families use \texttt{COOPERATE}/\texttt{DEFECT} and \texttt{RESTRAIN}/\texttt{OVERUSE} action pairs. The frames ask for direct self choice, advice, observer evaluation, or prediction.

The primary metric is dyadic cooperation:
\[
\mathrm{cooperation} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{cooperative}}{\#\mathrm{cooperative}+\#\mathrm{noncooperative}}.
\]
\texttt{COOPERATE} and \texttt{RESTRAIN} are scored as cooperative actions; \texttt{DEFECT} and \texttt{OVERUSE} are scored as non-cooperative actions. The frame, domain, game, variant, and presentation fields make prompt sensitivity measurable rather than implicit.

\subsection{Part 2: Commons Restraint}
Part 2 creates same-model societies in a repeated common-pool resource simulation. Each configured run starts with 50 active agents, a 100-day horizon, and a water reserve with capacity 2,500 units. \texttt{OPTION\_A} maps to \texttt{RESTRAIN}: it uses a 1x daily score multiplier and leaves the reserve unchanged. \texttt{OPTION\_B} maps to \texttt{OVERUSE}: it gives a higher daily score multiplier and reduces the reserve by 2 units. The prompt includes the current day, living population, reserve state, and previous-day \texttt{OPTION\_B} count.

The primary action metric is commons restraint:
\[
\mathrm{restraint} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{restrain}}{\#\mathrm{restrain}+\#\mathrm{overuse}}.
\]
Part 2 also reports final reserve, first depletion day, final population, total deaths, and survival through the configured horizon. This makes repeated local choices visible as aggregate population and resource trajectories.

\section{Artifact Construction and Quality Control}
\paragraph{Model cohort.}
The pilot evaluates 13 local/open-weight variants through Ollama. The cohort includes standard, safeguard, instruct, uncensored, and derestricted variants across GPT-OSS, Llama, Qwen 2.5, and Qwen 3.5 families. The cohort is intentionally useful for benchmark validation because it spans safety-oriented and less-restricted release styles, producing variation across all three parts.

\paragraph{Output contracts and metadata.}
Part 0 stores model outputs and a normalized compliance verdict. Part 1 requires a valid action and justification. Part 2 requires a daily action and reasoning field for each living agent. Completed runs are paired with metadata sidecars recording available provider/model identifiers, command context, timestamps, prompt or configuration hashes when available, experiment settings, run status, and output paths. Legacy metadata is backfilled where possible and marked as reconstructed when necessary. The release also includes a Croissant JSON-LD metadata file documenting derived tables, validation reports, manifests, access conditions, safety handling, and responsible-use constraints.

\paragraph{Validation.}
Validation is graph-independent. It checks CSV headers, duplicate rows, missing required fields, invalid actions, prompt-matrix coverage, Part 2 day continuity, incomplete days, reserve and population transition consistency, interrupted versus complete status, and reasoning/action mismatch flags. The current validation report covers 27 CSV files: one Part 0 file, 13 Part 1 files, and 13 Part 2 files. It reports 14 passes, 13 files with warning-level mismatch findings, and 0 failures. Those findings are retained because they are a measured diagnostic output of Part 2.

\paragraph{Harmful-output handling.}
Part 0 evaluates harmful prompts and may produce unsafe completions. The anonymous supplement therefore emphasizes derived tables, validation outputs, and aggregate summaries rather than raw harmful generations. This release choice preserves the evidence used in the paper while limiting unnecessary redistribution of harmful text.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
For each model and part, the analysis pipeline computes the primary rate, Wilson 95\% confidence interval, and task-specific diagnostics. Part 2 additionally computes first depletion day, final reserve, final population, total deaths, and reasoning/action mismatch count. Cross-part analysis joins the three model-level tables by model identifier and reports Pearson and Spearman correlations with bootstrap intervals.

The reported comparisons are descriptive pilot results over 13 related local/open-weight variants. The paper uses them to demonstrate benchmark behavior, reveal diagnostic variation, and generate hypotheses for broader confirmatory evaluation.

\paragraph{Refusal separates safety-oriented and less-restricted variants.}
Part 0 refusal ranges from 9.4\% to 99.3\%. Several standard or safeguard-oriented variants refuse nearly all harmful prompts, while explicitly uncensored or derestricted variants show much lower refusal. This gives the benchmark a strong safety axis for cross-part comparison.

\paragraph{Cooperation is strongly frame-sensitive.}
Part 1 cooperation ranges from 24.2\% to 75.0\%. The most informative result is not only the model ranking, but the frame effect: across models, cooperation is 83.2\% under observer-evaluation prompts, 66.8\% under advice prompts, 61.4\% under self-direct prompts, and 4.9\% under prediction prompts. The benchmark therefore distinguishes socially preferred judgments from expectations about how an agent will behave.

\paragraph{Commons restraint produces visible survival differences.}
Part 2 restraint ranges from 18.6\% to 92.5\%. Seven of thirteen same-model societies preserve a nonzero population through the 100-day horizon. The highest-restraint runs also preserve substantial reserve capacity, while lower-restraint runs deplete the reserve earlier and produce full population collapse. The environment turns repeated action choices into auditable resource and population trajectories.

\section{Diagnostics}
\paragraph{The constructs dissociate.}
The cross-part results support separate reporting. Safety refusal and dyadic cooperation have a weak association in this cohort (Pearson $r=0.24$, bootstrap interval $[-0.61,0.70]$). Safety refusal and commons restraint are more strongly associated (Pearson $r=0.77$, interval $[0.51,0.90]$). Cooperation and restraint are positive but uncertain (Pearson $r=0.34$, interval $[-0.25,0.94]$). These results show why the artifact reports multiple axes rather than a single aggregate readiness score.

\paragraph{Framing changes measured cooperation.}
Part 1 treats prompt frame as an experimental variable. Observer-evaluation prompts elicit high cooperative judgments, advice and self-direct prompts are intermediate, and prediction prompts elicit low cooperative predictions. This split is useful for auditing because a model can know which action is socially preferred while predicting that agents will act selfishly.

\paragraph{Restraint predicts survival in the simulated commons.}
Part 2 ties local overuse choices to aggregate outcomes. Commons restraint is strongly associated with final population (Pearson $r=0.85$, bootstrap interval $[0.68,0.98]$). The relationship is expected from the transition rules and useful for auditing: the benchmark makes cumulative overuse visible as resource depletion and population loss.

\paragraph{Reasoning/action mismatch is measurable.}
The validation pipeline flags \texttt{OVERUSE} rows whose reasoning describes the action as preserving the reserve, keeping the group safe, or beneficially triggering population loss. Across Part 2, it flags 823 rows. These conservative pattern checks expose a concrete process-level signal: a model can emit valid JSON while giving a rationale that conflicts with the selected action's incentives.

\section{Scope and Responsible Use}
Prosocial Readiness Bench is an evaluation artifact for observable actions and justifications. Its current release is strongest as a reproducible open-model pilot and benchmark package. The model cohort is local/open-weight, several variants are related, and local/Ollama tags can vary across installations unless exact revisions or digests are frozen. These factors affect leaderboard-style interpretation, but they do not affect the artifact contribution: the task contracts, schemas, validation checks, and analysis pipeline remain reusable for broader model cohorts.

The three benchmark parts also define clear scope boundaries. Part 0 uses automated compliance judgments that should be further calibrated for borderline, multilingual, or refusal-style cases. Part 1 evaluates artificial one-shot scenarios, making it well suited for controlled frame and domain comparisons but not a substitute for observing real institutions. Part 2 uses homogeneous same-model societies and one resource dynamic, making action-to-outcome trajectories easy to audit while leaving heterogeneous agents, institutions, shocks, and richer incentives for future extensions. Reasoning text is treated as process evidence, and the mismatch audit is a conservative pattern-based diagnostic rather than a complete semantic verifier.

The safety component requires careful release handling because harmful prompts can elicit unsafe completions. The anonymous supplement therefore provides the evidence needed for the paper's aggregate claims while limiting redistribution of raw harmful generations. The benchmark should be used to identify behavioral risks and compare mitigation strategies, not to improve harmful compliance.

\section{Conclusion}
Prosocial Readiness Bench provides a concrete, reproducible way to evaluate three observable behaviors that matter for LLM agents in social settings: refusing harmful requests, cooperating in dyadic dilemmas, and preserving a shared resource under repeated incentive conflict. The pilot shows that these axes reveal different model profiles, that prompt frame substantially changes measured cooperation, and that commons restraint is closely tied to simulated survival. The release packages the prompts, schemas, metadata, validation checks, tables, figures, and reproduction commands needed to audit and extend the benchmark. A natural next step is a frozen confirmatory evaluation with broader model coverage, repeated decoding settings, stronger judge calibration, richer commons environments, and preregistered cross-part hypotheses.
