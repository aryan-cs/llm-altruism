\begin{abstract}
We present a reproducible evaluation framework for comparing three observable behaviors in language-model agents: safety refusal under harmful requests, cooperation in one-shot social dilemmas, and restraint in repeated common-pool resource simulations. The contribution is an evaluation artifact and pilot audit rather than a claim that models have moral agency. We evaluate 13 local/open-weight model variants across three parts: a harmful-request refusal audit, a factorial dyadic-game benchmark, and a same-model commons simulation. The released artifact includes executable experiment code, durable run metadata, validation reports, summary tables, and an anonymous supplement. In the current pilot, model behavior varies substantially across constructs: top safety-refusal rates exceed 98\%, top dyadic cooperation rates are 75\%, and six of thirteen commons societies preserve a nonzero population through the configured horizon. Validation finds no file-level failures, but all 13 Part 2 runs contain warning-level reasoning/action mismatch flags where models choose overuse while describing the action as group-preserving. These warnings are treated as an empirical failure mode, not as noise to hide. We frame the benchmark as a cautious open-model audit of measurable behaviors: safety refusal, cooperation, restraint, resource preservation, and survival.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Language-model agents are increasingly evaluated in settings where they make choices with consequences for other agents. Standard helpfulness and accuracy tests do not capture several behaviors that matter in these settings: refusing harmful assistance, cooperating when defection is privately tempting, and preserving a shared resource when overuse creates collective risk. These behaviors are sometimes discussed under the broad label of ``altruism,'' but that term can imply intent, stable preference, or moral agency. This work instead studies observable behavior under controlled prompts.

We target the NeurIPS Evaluations and Datasets track because the main contribution is an evaluation protocol and executable artifact. The paper asks two questions. First, can safety refusal, cooperation, and commons restraint be measured in a reproducible pipeline with explicit metadata and validation? Second, in a pilot set of local/open-weight models, do the measured behaviors move together, or do they expose distinct failure modes?

The contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A three-part evaluation framework covering harmful-request refusal, dyadic social dilemmas, and repeated commons preservation.
    \item A validation and analysis pipeline that checks schemas, coverage, transitions, metadata, and reasoning/action consistency independently of plots.
    \item A pilot audit of 13 local/open-weight model variants, including warning-level evidence that some models choose overuse while rationalizing the choice as group-preserving.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
Safety-refusal benchmarks such as HarmBench and JailbreakBench evaluate whether models comply with harmful requests or resist jailbreak attempts \citep{mazeika2024harmbench,chao2024jailbreakbench}. Our Part 0 uses this line of work as the safety-refusal component of a broader behavioral audit.

The social-dilemma components draw on classic work on cooperation, prisoner's dilemmas, and common-pool resources \citep{axelrod1984evolution,hardin1968tragedy,ostrom1990governing}. Recent LLM-agent work has shown that language models can participate in simulated social environments and role-based interaction settings \citep{park2023generative}. Our emphasis is narrower: we do not claim to model real institutions, but instead use controlled prompt matrices to expose whether choices, justifications, and aggregate outcomes are internally consistent.

The E\&D framing also follows work that treats evaluation itself as an artifact. The benchmark is useful only if reviewers can inspect the protocol, reproduce tables, and see where warnings arise. For this reason, validation reports and manifests are first-class outputs rather than byproducts.

\section{Evaluation Framework}
\subsection{Part 0: Safety Refusal}
Part 0 presents harmful-request prompts and records whether a model refuses or materially complies. The primary metric is the safety-refusal rate:
\[
\mathrm{refusal} = \frac{\#\mathrm{denied}}{\#\mathrm{denied} + \#\mathrm{complied}}.
\]
The stored CSV schema includes provider, model, language, prompt source, prompt text, model reasoning when available, response, judge verdict, and a normalized compliance field. Part 0 raw generations are treated conservatively in release packaging because they may contain harmful content; aggregate summaries and validation outputs are included in the supplement.

\subsection{Part 1: Dyadic Cooperation}
Part 1 evaluates one-shot choices in two game families: prisoner's dilemma and temptation-of-the-commons analogues. The prompt matrix varies game, frame, domain, scenario variant, and presentation. The primary metric is cooperation rate, where \texttt{COOPERATE} and \texttt{RESTRAIN} are cooperative actions and \texttt{DEFECT} and \texttt{OVERUSE} are non-cooperative actions:
\[
\mathrm{cooperation} = \frac{\#\mathrm{cooperative}}{\#\mathrm{cooperative} + \#\mathrm{noncooperative}}.
\]

\subsection{Part 2: Commons Restraint}
Part 2 creates same-model societies in which agents repeatedly choose between \texttt{RESTRAIN} and \texttt{OVERUSE}. The primary metrics are restraint rate, first reserve-depletion day, final reserve, final population, deaths, and survival through the configured horizon. Each model run in the current pilot uses 50 agents, a 100-day horizon, and a water-reserve framing. We additionally audit reasoning/action consistency by flagging cases in which a model selects \texttt{OVERUSE} while describing the choice as preserving the reserve, keeping the group safe, or beneficially triggering population loss.

\section{Validation and Reproducibility}
The repository uses a single data root. Raw CSVs and metadata live under \texttt{data/raw/}; graph-independent validation reports, manifests, and tables live under \texttt{data/analysis/}; figures live under \texttt{data/graphs/}. Each completed run has a metadata sidecar with run status, provider, exact model identifier, command context, timestamp, prompt/config hash when available, experiment parameters, backend, timeout/token settings when available, and output paths. Legacy metadata is backfilled from filenames and configs where possible, with unknown fields marked explicitly.

Validation checks are independent of plotting. They cover CSV headers, duplicate keys, missing actions, invalid actions, prompt-matrix row counts, Part 2 day continuity, reserve/population transition consistency, interrupted versus completed runs, and reasoning/action mismatch flags. The current validation report covers 27 CSVs: one Part 0 file, 13 Part 1 files, and 13 Part 2 files. It reports 14 passes, 13 warnings, and 0 failures. All warning-level files are Part 2 runs with reasoning/action mismatch flags. These warnings remain in the paper because they are relevant to the failure mode being studied.

The main reproduction commands are:
\begin{verbatim}
uv run pytest -q
uv run python -m analysis.validation --strict
uv run python -m analysis.summarize_results
uv run python -m analysis.build_manifest
\end{verbatim}
The same commands are listed in \texttt{REPRODUCIBILITY.md}. Summary tables are written to \path{data/analysis/tables/}, and the manifest is written to \path{data/analysis/run_manifest.jsonl}.

\subsection{Safety Refusal}
Part 0 contains 13 model summaries with 297 judged prompts per model. Figure~\ref{fig:part0-refusal} shows large variation by model family and release style. Refusal is high for several standard or safeguard-oriented models and much lower for explicitly uncensored or derestricted variants. \subsection{Dyadic Cooperation}
Part 1 contains 384 prompt variants per model. Figure~\ref{fig:part1-cooperation} shows that the highest observed cooperation rate is 75.0\%, shared by three Qwen 2.5 variants, while several uncensored or derestricted variants have cooperation rates below 30\%. This should not be read as a monotonic safety-to-cooperation relationship: the pilot set is small, model families differ in many ways, and Part 1 uses a distinct behavioral construct.

\subsection{Commons Restraint}
Part 2 shows both aggregate survival differences and justification failures. Six of thirteen societies preserve a nonzero population through day 100; seven collapse to zero population. Figure~\ref{fig:part2-restraint} shows the model-level restraint rates. The two Llama variants and several Qwen variants preserve nonzero populations, while lower-restraint runs deplete the reserve and collapse earlier. Across Part 2, the reasoning/action audit flags 835 \texttt{OVERUSE} rows where the model's explanation describes the action as group-preserving or reserve-preserving.

\subsection{Cross-Part Interpretation}
The pilot supports a cautious conclusion: the three metrics are separable. Figures~\ref{fig:cross-safety-cooperation} and~\ref{fig:cross-cooperation-restraint} show two readable individual cross-part scatterplots; the full set of individual cross-part plots is included in the supplement. Safety refusal has a weak and uncertain relation to dyadic cooperation in this cohort (Pearson $r=0.24$), while safety refusal/restraint and cooperation/restraint are more positive but still pilot-scale comparisons over only 13 models. The strongest relation is restraint versus final population (Pearson $r=0.87$), which is expected because restraint is mechanistically tied to resource survival in the Part 2 environment. These patterns argue against collapsing the benchmark into a single ``good behavior'' score and motivate a larger confirmatory run with frozen model taxonomy, repeated seeds or decoding settings, and bootstrap uncertainty over model-level comparisons.

\section{Limitations}
This is an open-model pilot, not a definitive benchmark of frontier systems. The current results use local/Ollama model tags, which can drift across installations. The Part 2 societies are homogeneous same-model simulations; real deployments may contain heterogeneous agents, external incentives, and richer institutions. Metrics are prompt-sensitive and decoding-sensitive. Part 0 relies on judge labels and should be audited for borderline cases, multilingual outputs, and any cases where refusal style masks actionable harmful content. The reasoning/action mismatch audit is intentionally conservative and pattern-based; it catches salient contradictions but is not a complete semantic verifier.

\section{Ethics and Safety}
The harmful-request component creates and stores potentially unsafe generations. The anonymous supplement therefore includes Part 0 aggregate tables and validation reports, but excludes raw Part 0 completions by default. This limits unnecessary redistribution of harmful content while preserving the numerical claims used in the paper. The released artifact is intended for evaluation research and should not be used to optimize harmful compliance. The paper makes no claim that models possess moral agency or intrinsic altruism.

\section{Conclusion}
We introduced a reproducible evaluation framework for measuring safety refusal, dyadic cooperation, and commons restraint in LLM agents. The pilot shows strong variation across model variants, clear survival differences in commons simulations, and a concrete reasoning/action mismatch failure mode. The next step is a frozen confirmatory evaluation with broader model coverage, repeated settings, and final figure exports generated from the same graph-independent tables.
