\title{Social Restraint Bench: Measuring Safety Refusal, Cooperation, and Commons Preservation in LLM Agents}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{abstract}
Existing LLM safety benchmarks measure harmful-request refusal, while agent and social-dilemma evaluations often study cooperation or interaction separately. This separation makes it difficult to test whether a model that refuses harmful requests also cooperates in one-shot dilemmas or preserves shared resources in repeated settings. We introduce Social Restraint Bench, an executable behavioral benchmark that separates three observable constructs: safety refusal, dyadic cooperation, and commons restraint. The current artifact contains 3,861 harmful-request judgments, 4,992 dyadic social-dilemma decisions, and 47,529 commons-simulation agent-day decisions across 13 local/open-weight model variants. The pilot evaluation shows that these constructs are related but not interchangeable: safety refusal and dyadic cooperation have weak model-level association in this cohort, repeated commons restraint is strongly tied to survival in the simulation, and all Part 2 runs expose reasoning/action mismatch flags where models select overuse while describing the choice as preserving the group or reserve. We release prompts, schemas, metadata sidecars, validation checks, manifests, derived tables, figures, and reproduction commands, while excluding raw harmful completions from the default anonymous supplement. The benchmark measures observable behavior only; it is not evidence of model moral agency or intrinsic altruism.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
LLM agents are increasingly evaluated in settings where their choices affect other agents, users, or shared resources. Three behaviors matter in such settings: refusing harmful assistance, cooperating when defection is privately tempting, and preserving a shared resource when overuse gives short-term individual gain. Existing evaluations usually treat these behaviors separately. Safety benchmarks test whether models comply with harmful requests; social-dilemma experiments test one-shot cooperation; agent simulations test behavior in interactive environments. A single safety or helpfulness score can therefore hide separable failures: a model may refuse harmful requests but defect in dyadic games, cooperate in one-shot prompts but overuse a repeated commons, or choose the harmful commons action while justifying it as group-preserving.

This paper introduces Social Restraint Bench, a benchmark for measuring these separable behaviors under one reproducible artifact. The benchmark has three parts. Part 0 measures harmful-request refusal using harmful behavior prompts and a compliance judge. Part 1 measures one-shot dyadic cooperation across games, frames, domains, scenario variants, and presentation styles. Part 2 measures repeated commons restraint in same-model societies where each agent repeatedly chooses between a reserve-preserving action and a higher short-term payoff action that depletes a shared resource. The central design choice is to keep the constructs separate and then study cross-part covariation rather than collapse them into a single ``good behavior'' score.

The artifact is designed around three principles. First, every construct has an explicit task contract, output format, and primary metric. Second, the release treats validation as part of the benchmark: raw CSVs are paired with metadata sidecars, a manifest, graph-independent summary tables, and checks for schema, coverage, transition consistency, and reasoning/action mismatch flags. Third, the benchmark reports process-level failures, not only aggregate rates. In the commons simulation, for example, selecting \texttt{OVERUSE} while explaining the action as preserving the reserve is a measured failure mode rather than filtered noise.

The pilot evaluation covers 13 local/open-weight model variants. It is descriptive rather than definitive, but it demonstrates the benchmark's diagnostic value. Safety refusal varies from 9.4\% to 99.3\%; dyadic cooperation varies from 24.2\% to 75.0\%; and seven of thirteen same-model societies preserve a nonzero population through the 100-day horizon. Model-level safety refusal and dyadic cooperation have only weak association in this cohort (Pearson $r=0.24$), while commons restraint is strongly related to final population survival (Pearson $r=0.85$). Across Part 2, validation flags 823 cases in which models choose the reserve-depleting action while giving a group- or reserve-preserving rationale.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Benchmark artifact.} We introduce Social Restraint Bench, a three-part evaluation suite for safety refusal, one-shot cooperation, and repeated commons restraint in LLM agents.
    \item \textbf{Task contracts and metrics.} We define explicit input/output contracts, primary scores, and diagnostic checks for harmful-request refusal, dyadic social dilemmas, and repeated common-pool resource simulations.
    \item \textbf{Validated pilot dataset.} We report a pilot over 13 local/open-weight model variants, covering 56,382 total decisions or judgments and exporting graph-independent tables with Wilson intervals and bootstrap cross-part correlations.
    \item \textbf{Release package.} We provide prompts, schemas, experiment code, metadata sidecars, validation reports, manifests, analysis scripts, derived tables, and figures, with raw harmful completions redacted from the default anonymous supplement for safety.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work and Gap Matrix}
Safety-refusal benchmarks such as HarmBench and JailbreakBench evaluate whether models comply with harmful requests or resist jailbreak attempts \citep{mazeika2024harmbench,chao2024jailbreakbench}. These resources are essential for measuring harmful compliance, but they do not test whether refusal behavior transfers to cooperation or repeated resource restraint. Social-dilemma theory provides the conceptual basis for cooperation and common-pool resource problems \citep{axelrod1984evolution,hardin1968tragedy,ostrom1990governing}; our contribution is an executable LLM-agent benchmark rather than a new theory of cooperation. Agent simulation work shows that language models can participate in interactive social settings \citep{park2023generative}, while trust and preference datasets show the value of multi-axis evaluation and participant-aware analysis \citep{wang2023decodingtrust,kirk2024prism,jiang2025artificialhivemind}. Social Restraint Bench combines these motivations into a scoped measurement artifact: refusal, one-shot cooperation, and repeated commons restraint are evaluated separately, then compared at the model level.

\subsection{Part 0: Safety Refusal}
Part 0 presents harmful-request prompts and records whether the model refuses or materially complies. Prompt sources include JailbreakBench harmful behaviors, HarmBench behavior prompts, and in-the-wild jailbreak wrappers composed with benchmark behavior prompts. The model is asked to provide a reasoning field and direct response field. A strict compliance judge returns JSON with a Boolean compliance verdict. The primary score is:
\[
\mathrm{refusal} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{denied}}{\#\mathrm{denied}+\#\mathrm{complied}}.
\]
The stored schema includes provider, model, language, prompt source, prompt text, model reasoning when available, response, judge verdict, and normalized compliance. Because Part 0 may contain harmful completions, the default anonymous supplement includes aggregate tables and validation outputs but not raw harmful generations.

\subsection{Part 1: Dyadic Cooperation}
Part 1 evaluates one-shot social-dilemma choices. The prompt matrix has two game families, four frames, six domains, four scenario variants per domain-game pair, and two presentation styles, yielding 384 decisions per evaluated model. The game families are a prisoner's-dilemma form with \texttt{COOPERATE}/\texttt{DEFECT} actions and a temptation-of-the-commons form with \texttt{RESTRAIN}/\texttt{OVERUSE} actions. The frames ask for direct self choice, advice, observer evaluation, or prediction. Domains include crime, sports, workplace, healthcare, education, and neighborhood settings. The primary score is:
\[
\mathrm{cooperation} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{cooperative}}{\#\mathrm{cooperative}+\#\mathrm{noncooperative}}.
\]
\texttt{COOPERATE} and \texttt{RESTRAIN} are cooperative actions; \texttt{DEFECT} and \texttt{OVERUSE} are non-cooperative actions.

\subsection{Part 2: Commons Restraint}
Part 2 creates same-model societies in a repeated common-pool resource simulation. Each configured pilot run starts with 50 active agents, a 100-day horizon, a water reserve with capacity 2,500 units, and daily independent choices. \texttt{OPTION\_A} uses a 1x daily score multiplier and changes the shared reserve by 0 units. \texttt{OPTION\_B} uses a higher daily multiplier and reduces the reserve by 2 units. In analysis, \texttt{OPTION\_A} maps to \texttt{RESTRAIN} and \texttt{OPTION\_B} maps to \texttt{OVERUSE}. The primary score is:
\[
\mathrm{restraint} =
\frac{\#\mathrm{restrain}}{\#\mathrm{restrain}+\#\mathrm{overuse}}.
\]
Additional outcomes are first depletion day, final reserve, final population, total deaths, and survival through the configured horizon. The prompt includes current day, living agents, reserve state, and previous-day \texttt{OPTION\_B} count. This makes Part 2 a repeated environment rather than a one-shot preference prompt.

\section{Artifact Construction and Quality Control}
\paragraph{Model cohort.}
The current pilot evaluates 13 local/open-weight variants through Ollama. The cohort includes standard, safeguard, instruct, uncensored, and derestricted variants across GPT-OSS, Llama, Qwen 2.5, and Qwen 3.5 families. The model registry records exact provider-facing identifiers and family/style labels. We do not treat this cohort as a frontier-model ranking; it is a validation pass showing that the artifact runs end-to-end and exposes separable behaviors.

\paragraph{Output contracts.}
Each part uses a constrained output format. Part 0 uses a model response plus a judge JSON verdict. Part 1 requires JSON with one valid action and a short justification. Part 2 requires a single JSON object with exactly an action and brief reasoning. Validation checks invalid actions and missing required fields rather than silently coercing them.

\paragraph{Metadata and manifests.}
Completed runs have metadata sidecars recording provider, model identifier, command context, timestamp, prompt/config hash when available, experiment parameters, backend settings when available, run status, and output paths. Legacy files are backfilled where possible and marked explicitly when metadata is reconstructed. The manifest maps raw CSVs to metadata and derived analysis outputs.

\paragraph{Validation.}
Validation is graph-independent. It checks CSV headers, duplicate rows, missing required fields, invalid actions, Part 1 prompt-matrix coverage, Part 2 day continuity, incomplete days, reserve/population transition consistency, interrupted versus complete status, and reasoning/action mismatch flags. The current validation report covers 27 CSV files: one Part 0 file, 13 Part 1 files, and 13 Part 2 files. It reports 14 passes, 13 warnings, and 0 failures. All warnings are Part 2 reasoning/action mismatch flags, which are retained because they are a benchmarked failure mode.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
For each model and part, the analysis pipeline computes the primary rate, Wilson 95\% confidence interval, and task-specific diagnostics. Part 2 additionally computes first depletion day, final reserve, final population, total deaths, and reasoning/action mismatch count. Cross-part analysis joins the three model-level tables by model identifier and reports Pearson and Spearman correlations with bootstrap intervals. These correlations are descriptive because the pilot has only 13 model variants and the variants are not independent samples from a well-defined model population.

The intended interpretation is diagnostic. High performance on one part should not be interpreted as general ``alignment'' unless it also holds under the other task contracts. Conversely, low performance in one part identifies a specific failure mode: harmful compliance, one-shot non-cooperation, repeated overuse, collapse, or reasoning/action mismatch.

\section{Diagnostics}
\subsection{Construct Separability}
The pilot argues against collapsing the three parts into one score. Safety refusal and dyadic cooperation have weak association in this cohort (Pearson $r=0.24$, bootstrap interval $[-0.61,0.70]$). Safety refusal and commons restraint are more strongly associated (Pearson $r=0.77$, interval $[0.51,0.90]$), while cooperation and restraint are modest and uncertain (Pearson $r=0.34$, interval $[-0.25,0.94]$). These wide intervals reflect the small model cohort, but the direction is clear enough for benchmark design: the three constructs should be reported separately.

\subsection{Commons Survival}
Part 2 supplies a mechanistic diagnostic absent from one-shot prompts: overuse affects the shared reserve and therefore population survival. Restraint is strongly associated with final population (Pearson $r=0.85$, interval $[0.68,0.98]$). This correlation is expected because the environment directly ties overuse to depletion, but it is still useful: the benchmark turns a local action preference into an aggregate trajectory that can collapse or survive.

\subsection{Reasoning/Action Mismatch}
The validation pipeline flags \texttt{OVERUSE} rows whose reasoning describes the action as preserving the reserve, keeping the group safe, or beneficially triggering population loss. Across Part 2, it flags 823 rows. The highest mismatch count in the pilot is 164 for one derestricted GPT-OSS variant; the lowest is 6 for one Qwen 2.5 abliteration variant. These flags are conservative pattern checks, not a complete semantic verifier. They nevertheless expose a useful failure mode: the selected action and stated rationale can disagree even when the JSON action is valid.

\section{Release, Reproducibility, and Intended Use}
The release is organized so paper claims can be traced to files. Experiment code lives under \path{experiments/part0}, \path{experiments/part1}, and \path{experiments/part2}. Analysis code lives under \path{analysis/}. Raw CSVs and metadata live under \path{data/raw/}; derived tables, validation reports, and manifests live under \path{data/analysis/}; figures live under \path{data/graphs/}. Release documentation is provided in \texttt{REPRODUCIBILITY.md}, \texttt{DATA\_CARD.md}, and \texttt{MODEL\_REGISTRY.md}.

The main reproduction commands are:
\begin{verbatim}
uv run pytest -q
uv run python -m analysis.validation --strict
uv run python -m analysis.summarize_results
uv run python -m analysis.build_manifest
\end{verbatim}
Figure regeneration commands are documented separately because figures are intentionally downstream of graph-independent validation and table generation.

The intended use is behavioral evaluation research: comparing model variants, prompt settings, and agent designs under explicit task contracts. Out-of-scope uses include claiming that models possess moral agency, using the benchmark to optimize harmful compliance, or treating the pilot cohort as a definitive ranking of frontier systems.

\section{Limitations, Ethics, and Safety}
The current evaluation is a pilot over local/open-weight model variants, not a definitive benchmark of frontier systems. The model variants are not independent samples, and local/Ollama tags can drift across installations unless exact revisions or digests are frozen. Part 1 is prompt-sensitive and evaluates artificial one-shot scenarios rather than real institutions. Part 2 uses homogeneous same-model societies with a simplified resource dynamic; real deployments may contain heterogeneous agents, richer institutions, exogenous shocks, and incentives not captured here. The reasoning/action mismatch audit is pattern-based and conservative; it should be replaced or supplemented by stronger semantic verification before drawing fine-grained claims about explanations.

Part 0 creates and stores potentially unsafe generations. The default anonymous supplement therefore excludes raw harmful completions while retaining aggregate tables, validation reports, and derived claims. Before public release, upstream prompt-source licenses and harmful-output handling should be reviewed again. The benchmark measures observable behavior under prompts and simulations. It does not establish intent, stable preference, moral agency, or intrinsic altruism.

\section{Conclusion}
Social Restraint Bench turns a broad question about socially beneficial LLM-agent behavior into a reproducible evaluation artifact with explicit task contracts. The pilot shows why the separation matters: safety refusal, dyadic cooperation, and commons restraint do not reduce to one score, repeated overuse can produce population collapse, and valid actions can be paired with contradictory rationales. The next step is a frozen confirmatory evaluation with broader model coverage, repeated decoding settings, stronger judge calibration, and additional ablations over prompt framing and commons incentives.
